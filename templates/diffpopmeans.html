Comparing Two Population Means
(variances equal but unknown)

Learning Objectives:

  • Calculate confidence Interval (CI) for the difference of two population means, $\mu_1 - \mu_2$
  • Perform hypothesis testing for the difference of two population means, $\mu_1 - \mu_2$
    1. If $\mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0$, two-tailed test
    2. If $\mu_1 - \mu_2 < 0$, left-tailed test
    3. If $\mu_1 - \mu_2 > 0$, right-tailed test

Introduction:

We compare means of two populations using independent samples. We consider two independent simple random samples from two populations and apply these samples and statistical tools to test whether the mean of a population 1 is equal to the mean of population 2. We also estimate the confidence inteval for the difference to means. We assume that the standard deviations are equal but unknown. We also assume that the populations are normally distributed or the sample sizes are large enough to apply the central limit theorem.

We use the following notations:

    Population 1:

  • Sample size = \(n_1\)
  • Sample mean = \(\bar{x}_1\)
  • Sample standard deviation = \(s_1\)
  • Population mean = \(\mu_1\)
  • Population standard deviation = \(\sigma_1\) (unknown)

    Population 2:

  • Sample size = \(n_2\)
  • Sample mean = \(\bar{x}_2\)
  • Sample standard deviation = \(s_2\)
  • Population mean = \(\mu_2\)
  • Population standard deviation = \(\sigma_2\) (unknown)

We consider the random variable \(\bar{X_1} - \bar{X_2}\) whose mean estimates the difference in population means \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\). For large sample sizes, the variable \(\bar{X_1} - \bar{X_2}\) follows a normal distribution with mean equal to \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\) and the standard deviation equal to \(\displaystyle \sqrt{\frac{\sigma_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{\sigma_2^2}{n_2}}\).
We consider the population standard deviations are equal but unknown, \(\sigma_1 = \sigma_2 = \sigma\). We estimate the common population standard deviation using the sample standard deviations.
Thus, the standard deviation of the random variable \(\bar{X_1} - \bar{X_2}\) is given by, \(\displaystyle \sqrt{\frac{\sigma_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{\sigma_2^2}{n_2}} = \sigma \sqrt{\left( \frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2} \right)}\) and $\sigma$ is estimated by the pooled sample standard deviation, $s_p$ which is calculated as, \(s_p = \displaystyle \sqrt{\frac{(n_1 - 1)s_1^2 + (n_2 - 1)s_2^2}{n_1 + n_2 - 2}}\):
Thus, the random variable,

$\displaystyle t = \frac{(\bar{X_1} - \bar{X_2}) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2)}{s_p\sqrt{\left( \frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2} \right)}}$ follows a t-distribution with \(n_1 + n_2 - 2\) degrees of freedom.

Confidence Interval for \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\):

We estimate the $(1-\alpha)100\%$ confidence interval for the difference of two population means, \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\) using the following formula:

  • Point Estimate of \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\) = \(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2\) with margin of error, \(ME = t_{\alpha/2}.s_p.\sqrt{\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}}\)
  • CI = \(\displaystyle \Bigg( (\bar{x_1} - \bar{x_2}) - t_{\alpha/2}.s_p.\sqrt{\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}}, \quad (\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) + t_{\alpha/2}.s_p.\sqrt{\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}} \Bigg)\)
    • or
  • CI = \(\Big( (\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - ME,\quad (\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) + ME \Big)\),
  • where \(t_{\alpha/2}\) is the critical value from the t-distribution with \(n_1 + n_2 - 2\) degrees of freedom.

Hypothesis Testing (pooled t-test:)

We state the hypothesis to prove that if two means are equal or one is greater than the other.

  1. Two-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu_1 = \mu_2\)     or     \(\mu_1-\mu_2 = 0\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu_1 \neq \mu_2\)     or     \(\mu_1 - \mu_2 \neq 0\)
  2. Left-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu_1 = \mu_2\)     or     \(\mu_1 = \mu_2\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu_1 < \mu_2\)     or     \(\mu_1-\mu_2 <0 \)
  3. Right-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu_1 = \mu_2\)     or     \(\mu_1 = \mu_2\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu_1 > \mu_2\)     or     \(\mu_1-\mu_2>0\)


Provide the following values:

  • Sample sizes: \(n_1, n_2\).
  • Degree of Freedom (DF) = \(n_1 + n_2 - 2\)
  • Sample means: \(\bar{x}_1, \bar{x}_2\)
  • Sample standard deviations: \(s_1, s_2\).
  • Population means: \(\mu_1, \mu_2\).
  • Significance level: \(\alpha\).

It computes and displays the following values:

  • Test statistic: \( t_{\text{test}} =\displaystyle \frac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2)}{s_p\sqrt{\left( \frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2} \right)}} = \displaystyle \frac{(\bar{x}_ - \bar{x}_2)}{s_p\sqrt{\left( \frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2} \right)}} \) (under the null hypothesis, (since \(\mu_1 = \mu_2 \)))
  • Critical value: \(t_{\text{crit}}\)
  • P-value: \(p\)

Conclusion:

  • Do not reject \(H_0\) if Test Statistic, \(t_{\text{test}}\) is in the non-rejection region. Else reject \(H_0\) in favor of \(H_a\).
    • OR
  • Do not reject \(H_0\) if P-Value is bigger than \(\alpha\). Else reject \(H_0\) in favor of \(H_a\).
