Introduction:

The normal distribution is a continuous probability distribution that is symmetric about its mean. Its density curve is bell-shaped and symmetric about its mean. The total area under the probability density curve equals 1, mathematically if $f(x)$ is the probability density function, then $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) dx = 1$. the normal distribution is characterized by two parameters: the mean (μ) and the standard deviation (σ). The mean determines the center of the distribution, while the standard deviation controls the spread of the distribution. The normal distribution is widely used in statistics and various fields to model real-world phenomena, such as heights, test scores, and measurement errors. It serves as a fundamental building block for many statistical methods and inference techniques.



Shape of the Density Curve:

The probability density function of a normal distribution with mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$ is given by: $$f(x) =\displaystyle \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$$ The graph of this density function is bell-shaped and symmetric around its mean. The mean (μ) determines the center of the curve, while the standard deviation (σ) controls its spread. Larger standard deviations result in wider curves, and smaller values produce narrower curves. No matter what the shape of the curve is, the area under the curve is always 1. This is mathematically expressed as $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) dx = 1$

Standard Normal Distribution

The normal distribution with mean 0 and standard deviation 1 is called the standard normal distribution. It is denoted by $Z$ and has the following probability density function: $$f(z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{z^2}{2}}$$ The standard normal distribution is a special case of the normal distribution and serves as a reference distribution for standardizing normal random variables. Any normal random variable $X$ with mean μ and standard deviation σ can be transformed into a standard normal random variable $Z$ using the formula: $$Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}$$ This transformation allows us to compute probabilities and percentiles for any normal distribution using the standard normal

The standard normal distribution is widely used in hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, and various statistical analyses. In all of these applications, we often need to find probabilities associated with the standard normal distribution.
