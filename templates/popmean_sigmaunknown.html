A Population Mean
(unknown variance)

Learning Objectives:

  • Calculate confidence Interval (CI) for the population mean, $\mu$
  • Calculate required sample size for a given margin of error and confidence level
  • Perform hypothesis testing for the population mean, $\mu$
    1. If $\mu = \mu_0$, two-tailed test
    2. If $\mu < \mu_0$, left-tailed test
    3. If $\mu > \mu_0$, right-tailed test

Introduction:

We consider a population with unknow mean unknown variance. We estimate the population mean, \(\mu\), using a sample drawn from the population. Since the population standard deviation, \(\sigma\), is unknown, we use the sample standard deviation, \(s\), to estimate it.

  1. Parameter Estimation: The sample mean is used to estimate the population mean. It is an unbiased estimator of \(\mu\) with a standard error of \( \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \), where \(n\) is the sample size. Sometimes finding a range of values is more important than a single point estimate. This is done by constructing a confidence interval in which the parameter lies.
  2. Hypothesis Testing: We test hypotheses about the population mean using the t-test when \(\sigma\) is unknown. The null hypothesis, \(H_0\), typically states that the population mean is equal to a specific value, \(\mu_0\). The alternative hypothesis, \(H_a\), can be two-tailed or one-tailed, depending on the research question.

Confidence Interval (CI):

Using sample data, we construct (1-\(\alpha\))100% confidence interval for the population mean, \(\mu\). If the significance level, \(\alpha\) is 0.05, then we are (1-0.05)100% = 95% confident that the interval contains the true population mean, \(\mu\). We interpret this as if we were to take many samples and construct confidence intervals for each sample, approximately 95% of those intervals would contain the true population mean, \(\mu\).

To construct the confidence interval (two-sided), we use the following steps:

  • $s$ = Sample standard deviation calculated from the sample data.
  • $\bar{x}$ =Sample mean calculated from the sample data.
  • $n$ = Sample size is the number of observations in the sample.
  • $df$ = Degree of freedom, calculated as \(n - 1\).
  • $\alpha$ = Significance level.
  • $t_{\alpha/2}$ = critical value for $\alpha$.

Then, we calculate the confidence interval using the formula:

  • $ \displaystyle CI = \left[\bar{x} - t_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}, \quad \bar{x} + t_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\right] $
  • Margin of Error (ME) is given by:
    $ ME = \displaystyle t_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} $
    Thus, the CI can also be expressed as:
  • $\displaystyle CI = \left[\bar{x} - ME, \quad \bar{x} + ME\right] $

Hypothesis Testing:

We perform hypothesis testing for a population mean when the population standard deviation, \(\sigma\), is unknown. We use the t-test to determine whether to reject or fail to reject the null hypothesis, \(H_0\), based on the sample data.

Provide the following values:

  • \(\bar{x}\) = Sample mean
  • \(\mu_0\) = Population mean
  • \(n\) = Sample size
  • \(s\) = Sample standard deviation
  • \(\alpha\) = Significance level

We perform the following testings:

  1. Two-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu = \mu_0\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu \neq \mu_0\)
    We find the following values for two-tailed test:
    • Test Statistic, \(T\) = \( \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}} \)
    • P-value: \( P(t > |T|) \) (area under the curve in both tails beyond \(\pm |T|\))
    • Critical Value: \(\pm t_{\alpha/2}\)
    • Non - rejection Region for \(\bar{x}\): \(\left(\mu_0 - \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}t_{\alpha/2}, \quad \mu_0 + \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}t_{\alpha/2}\right)\)
    • Non - rejection Region for \(T\): \(\left(-t_{\alpha/2}, \quad t_{\alpha/2}\right)\)
  2. Left-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu = \mu_0\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu < \mu_0\)
    We find the following values for left-tailed test:
    • Test Statistic, \(T\) = \( \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}} \)
    • P-value: \( P(T < t) \) (area under the curve to the left of \(T\))
    • Critical Value: -\( t_{\alpha} \)
    • Non - rejection Region for \(\bar{x}\): \( \left(\mu_0 - \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}t_{\alpha}, \quad \infty \right) \)
    • Non - rejection Region for \(T\): \( \left(-t_{\alpha}, \quad \infty \right) \)
  3. Right-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu = \mu_0\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu > \mu_0\)
    We find the following values for right-tailed test:
    • Test Statistic, \(T\) = \( \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}} \)
    • P-value: \( P(T > t) \) (area under the curve to the right of \(T\))
    • Critical Value: \( t_{\alpha} \)
    • Non - rejection Region for \(\bar{x}\): \( \left( -\infty, \quad \mu_0 + \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}t_{\alpha} \right) \)
    • Non - rejection Region for \(T\): \( \left( -\infty, \quad t_{\alpha} \right) \)

We also visualize the results of hypothesis testing by plotting the sampling distribution of the sample mean under the null hypothesis, \(H_0\). We shade the rejection region(s) based on the type of test and indicate the position of the sample mean, \(\bar{x}\), on the plot. This helps us understand how extreme our sample mean is in relation to the null hypothesis and whether it falls within the rejection region.
