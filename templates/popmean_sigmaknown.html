One Population Mean
(known variance)

Learning Objectives:

  • Calculate confidence Interval (CI) for the population mean, $\mu$
  • Calculate required sample size for a given margin of error and confidence level
  • Perform hypothesis testing for the population mean, $\mu$
    1. If $\mu = \mu_0$, two-tailed test
    2. If $\mu < \mu_0$, left-tailed test
    3. If $\mu > \mu_0$, right-tailed test

Introduction:

We consider a population with unknown mean $\mu$ and known standard deviation $\sigma$. We estimate the population mean and find a confidence interval. Then, we find various values related to hypothesis testing concerning \(\mu\). We perform these analyses under the assumption that the population standard deviation, \(\sigma\), is known.

  1. Parameter Estimation: The sample mean is used to estimate the population mean. It is an unbiased estimator of \(\mu\) with a standard error of \( \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \), where \(n\) is the sample size. Sometimes finding a range of values is more important than a single point estimate. This is done by constructing a confidence interval in which the parameter lies.
  2. Hypothesis Testing: We test hypotheses about the population mean using the Z-test when \(\sigma\) is known. The null hypothesis, \(H_0\), typically states that the population mean is equal to a specific value, \(\mu_0\). The alternative hypothesis, \(H_a\), can be two-tailed or one-tailed, depending on the research question.

Confidence Interval (CI)

Using sample data and population standard deviaiton, we construct (1-\(\alpha\))100% confidence interval for the population mean, \(\mu\). If the significance level, \(\alpha\) is 0.05, then we are (1-0.05)100% = 95% confident that the interval contains the true population mean, \(\mu\). We interpret this as if we were to take many samples and construct confidence intervals for each sample, approximately 95% of those intervals would contain the true population mean, \(\mu\).

To construct the confidence interval (two-sided), we use the following steps:

  • $\sigma$ = Population standard deviation given.
  • $\bar{x}$ =Sample mean calculated from the sample data.
  • $n$ = Sample size is the number of observations in the sample.
  • $\alpha$ = Significance level.
  • $z_{\alpha/2}$ = critical value for $\alpha$.

Then, we calculate the confidence interval using the formula:

  • $ \displaystyle CI = \left[\bar{x} - z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}, \quad \bar{x} + z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right] $
  • Margin of Error (ME) is given by:
    $ ME = \displaystyle z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} $
    Thus, the CI can also be expressed as:
  • $\displaystyle CI = \left[\bar{x} - ME, \quad \bar{x} + ME\right] $

Margin of Error and Sample size:

The margin of error (ME) represents the maximum expected difference between the sample mean and the true population mean within a specified confidence level. Smaller margin of error indicates a more precise estimate of the population mean, while a larger margin of error indicates less precision.
$ ME = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} $
Solving this equation for \(n\), we get
$ n = \displaystyle \left( \frac{z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sigma}{ME} \right)^2 $
This formula allows us to calculate the required sample size, \(n\), to achieve a desired margin of error, \(ME\), at a given confidence level, \(\alpha\).

Hypothesis Testing:

We perform hypothesis testing for a population mean when the population standard deviation, \(\sigma\), is known. We use the Z-test to determine whether to reject or fail to reject the null hypothesis, \(H_0\), based on the sample data.

Provide the following values:

  • \(\bar{x}\) = Sample mean
  • \(\mu_0\) = Population mean
  • \(n\) = Sample size
  • \(\sigma\) = Population standard deviation
  • \(\alpha\) = Significance level

We perform the following testings:

  1. Two-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu = \mu_0\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu \neq \mu_0\)
    We find the following values for two-tailed test:
    • Test Statistic, \(Z\) = \( \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \)
    • P-value: \( P(z > |Z|) \) (area under the curve in both tails beyond \(\pm |Z|\))
    • Critical Value: \(\pm z_{\alpha/2}\)
    • Non - rejection Region for \(\bar{x}\): \(\left(\mu_0 - \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}z_{\alpha/2}, \quad \mu_0 + \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}z_{\alpha/2}\right)\)
    • Non - rejection Region for \(Z\): \(\left(-z_{\alpha/2}, \quad z_{\alpha/2}\right)\)
  2. Left-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu = \mu_0\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu < \mu_0\)
    We find the following values for left-tailed test:
    • Test Statistic, \(Z\) = \( \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \)
    • P-value: \( P(Z > z) \) (area under the curve to the left of \(Z\))
    • Critical Value: -\( z_{\alpha} \)
    • Non - rejection Region for \(\bar{x}\): \( \left(\mu_0 - \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}z_{\alpha}, \quad \infty \right) \)
    • Non - rejection Region for \(Z\): \( \left(-z_{\alpha}, \quad \infty \right) \)
  3. Right-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu = \mu_0\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu > \mu_0\)
    We find the following values for right-tailed test:
    • Test Statistic, \(Z\) = \( \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \)
    • P-value: \( P(Z < z) \) (area under the curve to the right of \(Z\))
    • Critical Value: \( z_{\alpha} \)
    • Non - rejection Region for \(\bar{x}\): \( \left( -\infty, \quad \mu_0 + \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}z_{\alpha} \right) \)
    • Non - rejection Region for \(Z\): \( \left( -\infty, \quad z_{\alpha} \right) \)

We also visualize the results of hypothesis testing by plotting the sampling distribution of the sample mean under the null hypothesis, \(H_0\). We shade the rejection region(s) based on the type of test and indicate the position of the sample mean, \(\bar{x}\), on the plot. This helps us understand how extreme our sample mean is in relation to the null hypothesis and whether it falls within the rejection region.

Interpretation of Results:

  • If the sample mean, \(\bar{x}\), falls within the non-rejection region, we fail to reject the null hypothesis, \(H_0\). This means that there is not enough evidence to support the alternative hypothesis, \(H_a\).
  • If the sample mean, \(\bar{x}\), falls within the rejection region, we reject the null hypothesis, \(H_0\). This indicates that there is sufficient evidence to support the alternative hypothesis, \(H_a\).
  • The p-value helps us understand the strength of evidence against the null hypothesis. A smaller p-value indicates stronger evidence against \(H_0\).
  • The critical value(s) define the threshold(s) for rejecting \(H_0\). If our test statistic exceeds these critical value(s), we reject \(H_0\).
