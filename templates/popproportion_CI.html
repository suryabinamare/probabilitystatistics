A Population Proportion

Learning Objectives:

  • Use the sample proportion, \(\hat{p}\), as a point estimate for the population proportion, \(p\).
  • Construct a confidence interval for the population proportion.
  • Find the sample size, \(n\), required to achieve a desired margin of error for estimating the population proportion.
  • Perform hypothesis testing for a population proportion.

Introduction:

Suppose we have a sample of size \(n\) with \(x\) successes (or $x$ have a specified attribute). The sample proportion, \(\hat{p} = \displaystyle \frac{x}{n}\) is the ratio of successes in the sample. In orther words, a sample proportion is the ratio of values that hold a specified attribute divided by the sample size. The sample proportion, \(\hat{p}\), is a random variable that estimates the population proportion, \(p\).

The distribution of \(\hat{p}\) is approximately normal if the sample size is large enough, with mean \(\mu_{\hat{p}} = p\) and standard deviation \(\sigma_{\hat{p}} = \displaystyle \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}\). As \(p\) is unknown, we replace \(p\) with \(\hat{p}\). And for large sample size, $n$ and \(\hat{p}\) such that both \(n.\bar{p}\) and \(n.(1-\hat{p})\) is greater than 5, we use the distribution of \(\hat{p}\) which is approximately normal with mean equal to $p$ and standard deviation equal to $\displaystyle \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}$.

Point Estimate and Confidence Interval:

  • Calculate the sample proportion \(\hat{p} = \frac{x}{n}\), which is an unbiased point estimate of the population proportion.
  • Compute the standard error, \(se = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\).
  • Margin of error, \(ME = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot se\)
  • (\(1-\alpha\))% confidence interval:
    CI = \( \displaystyle \hat{p} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} \)
    or
    CI = \( \left(\hat{p} - z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}, \quad \hat{p} + z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\right)\).
    or
    CI = \(\qquad \left(\hat{p} - ME,\quad \hat{p} + ME\right) = \hat{p} \pm ME\)

Margin of Error (ME):

When the sample proportion is used to estimate the population proportion, the margin of error (ME) is given by the formula: \(ME = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\). ME quantifies the uncertainty associated with using a sample to estimate a population parameter. A smaller margin of error indicates a more precise estimate of the population proportion, while a larger margin of error indicates less precision. The margin of error is influenced by the confidence level (through \(z_{\alpha/2}\)), the variability in the data (through \(\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})\)), and the sample size (through \(n\)).

Solving for $n$, we get \(n = \displaystyle \frac{(z_{\alpha/2})^2 \cdot \hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{(ME)^2}\). This formula allows us to determine the required sample size, $n$, to achieve a desired margin of error, $ME$, for estimating the population proportion with a specified confidence level. But $\hat{p}$ is unknown since $\hat{p} = \displaystyle \frac{x}{n}$ is the sample proportion and we don't know $n$. We can use an estimate of $\hat{p}$ to find the required sample size.

  • Make an estimate of $\hat{p}$ based on prior knowledge or a pilot study.
  • If no estimate is available, use $\hat{p} = 0.5$ to maximize the required sample size. This is because the product $\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})$ is maximized when $\hat{p} = 0.5$, which gives the most conservative estimate for the required sample size.

Hypothesis Testing for a Proportion:

We perform hypothesis testing for a population proportion. We use the z-test to determine whether to reject or fail to reject the null hypothesis, \(H_0\), based on the sample data. The steps involved in hypothesis testing are as follows:

  1. Two-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad p = p_0\)        
    • \(H_a : \quad p \neq p_0\)        
  2. Left-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad p = p_0\)        
    • \(H_a : \quad p < p_0\)        
  3. Right-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad p = p_0\)        
    • \(H_a : \quad p > p_0\)        

Provide the following values:

  • Sample size: \(n\)
  • Sample Proportion: \(\hat{p}\)
  • Population Proportion: \(p_0\)
  • Significance Level: \(\alpha\)

We find the following values:

  • Test Statistic, \(z_{\text{test}}\) = \(\displaystyle \frac{\hat{p}-p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)/n}}\)
  • Critical Value: \(z_{\text{crit}}\)
  • P-Value:
    • \(= P(z < |z_{\text{test}}|) \) = area to the left and right of \(z_{\text{test}}\) (two-tailed test)
    • \(= P(z > z_{\text{test}}) \) = area to the right of \(z_{\text{test}}\) (right-tailed test)
    • \(= P(z < z_{\text{test}}) \) = area to the left of \(z_{\text{test}}\) (left-tailed test)

Conclusion:

  • Do not reject \(H_0\) if \(z_{\text{test}}\) is in the non-rejection region. Else reject \(H_0\) in favor of alternative hypothesis.
    • OR
  • Do not reject \(H_0\) if P-Value is bigger than \(\alpha\). Else reject \(H_0\) in favor of alternative hypothesis.
