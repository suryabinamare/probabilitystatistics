Introduction:

The Binomial Distribution is a discrete probability distribution that models the number of successes in a fixed number of independent Bernoulli trials, where each trial has only two outcomes (success or failure) with constant probability of success $p$. It is characterized by two parameters: the number of trials (n) and the probability of success in each trial (p). If a random variable $X$ represents the number of successes in $n$ trials, then $X$ follows a binomial distribution. The probability mass function (PMF) of the binomial distribution is given by: $$P(X = k) = C(n,k) \cdot p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$$ where $k$ is the number of successes (0 ≤ k ≤ n), and $\displaystyle C(n,k)= \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$ is the binomial coefficient, representing the number of ways to choose $k$ successes from $n$ trials.

The mean (expected value) and variance of a binomially distributed random variable $X$ are given by: $$\text{Mean} (\mu) = n \cdot p$$ $$\text{Variance} (\sigma^2) = n \cdot p \cdot (1 - p)$$ The binomial distribution is widely used in various fields, including statistics, finance, and quality control, to model scenarios involving binary outcomes, such as success/failure, yes/no, or pass/fail situations.



Histogram of Binomial Distribution

The shape of the binomial distribution curve depends on the values of the parameters n (number of trials) and p (probability of success). When p = 0.5, the distribution is symmetric around its mean. As p moves away from 0.5, the distribution becomes skewed. For small values of n, the distribution may appear discrete and jagged, while for larger values of n, it approaches a normal distribution shape due to the Central Limit Theorem. The binomial distribution curve represents the probabilities of obtaining different numbers of successes in a fixed number of trials. The height of each bar in the histogram corresponds to the probability of achieving that specific number of successes.
