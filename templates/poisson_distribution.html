Introduction:

The Poisson Distribution is a discrete probability distribution that models the number of events occurring in a fixed interval of time or space, assuming that these events occur independently and at a constant average rate $\lambda$. It is characterized by a single parameter: the average rate of occurrence $\lambda$. If a random variable $X$ represents the number of events in an interval, then $X$ follows a Poisson distribution. The probability mass function (PMF) of the Poisson distribution is given by: $$P(X = k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda} \lambda^k}{k!}$$ where $k$ is the number of events (0 ≤ k < ∞), and $\displaystyle e^{-\lambda}$ is the exponential function.

The mean (expected value) and variance of a Poisson-distributed random variable $X$ are given by: $$\text{Mean} (\mu) = \lambda$$ $$\text{Variance} (\sigma^2) = \lambda$$ The Poisson distribution is widely used in various fields, including statistics, finance, and quality control, to model scenarios involving count data, such as the number of customers arriving at a store or the number of defects in a product.



Histogram of the Poisson Distribution

The shape of the Poisson distribution curve depends on the value of the parameter $\lambda$ (the average rate of occurrence). For small values of $\lambda$, the distribution is skewed to the right, with a higher probability of observing fewer events. As $\lambda$ increases, the distribution becomes more symmetric and approaches a normal distribution. The histogram of the Poisson distribution represents the probabilities of observing different numbers of events in a fixed interval.
