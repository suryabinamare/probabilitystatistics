Chi-square Distribution

Learning Objectives:

  • Visualize the density curve of a chi-square distribution for different parameter values.
  • Find the p-value corresponding to a given chi-square value, $\chi^2$, and degree of freedom, df.
  • Find the $\chi^2$ value corresponding to a given p-value and degree of freedom, df.

Introduction:

The Chi-square distribution is a special case of the gamma distribution and is used primarily in hypothesis testing, particularly in tests of independence and goodness of fit. It is characterized by a single parameter, the degrees of freedom (df), which determines the shape of the distribution. The Chi-square distribution is right-skewed and takes only non-negative values.

  1. \(\chi^2\) - value: The value of the Chi-square statistic you want to evaluate. This value is plotted along the horizontal axis as the red dot.
  2. P-value: The probability of observing a value as extreme as the \(\chi^2\) - value. This value represents the area under the curve to the right of the \(\chi^2\) - value and is shaded blue in the plot.

Shape of the Distribution

The shape of the Chi-square distribution is determined by its degrees of freedom (df). When the degrees of freedom are small, the distribution is strongly skewed to the right, with a long tail extending toward larger values. As the degrees of freedom increase, the curve becomes less skewed, more balanced, and gradually begins to resemble a normal distribution.

For sufficiently large degrees of freedom, the Chi-square distribution appears nearly symmetric, although it always remains defined only for non-negative values.
